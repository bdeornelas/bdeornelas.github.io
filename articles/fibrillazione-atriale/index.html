What is atrial fibrillation?

Atrial fibrillation is a type of abnormal heart rhythm, also called "A-fib." In people with atrial fibrillation, the upper chambers of the heart beat irregularly and often very fast. Instead of contracting normally, the upper chambers quiver or fibrillate.

Normally, the heart beats in a steady rhythm controlled by electrical signals. In atrial fibrillation, abnormal electrical signals cause the upper chambers (atria) to contract very rapidly and irregularly, typically 300 to 600 times per minute.

Atrial fibrillation affects about 2 to 3 percent of people in developed countries. It becomes much more common with age. About 10 percent of people over 80 have atrial fibrillation. Over a lifetime, about 1 in 3 people will develop this condition.

What are the symptoms of atrial fibrillation?

Some people with atrial fibrillation have no symptoms. The condition is discovered during a routine exam or test. About 1 in 3 people with atrial fibrillation do not have symptoms.

When symptoms occur, they can include:

  • ●Palpitations (feeling that the heart is racing, beating irregularly, or "skipping beats")
  • ●Shortness of breath, especially during physical activity
  • ●Feeling very tired or weak
  • ●Chest discomfort or pressure, especially if the heart beats very fast
  • ●Dizziness or lightheadedness

When should I get emergency medical help?

GET EMERGENCY HELP (call emergency services or go to the emergency room) if you have:

  • ●Severe chest pain
  • ●Severe shortness of breath
  • ●Sudden weakness in an arm or leg
  • ●Difficulty speaking or seeing
  • ●Loss of consciousness or near-fainting

Why is atrial fibrillation serious?

Atrial fibrillation is serious mainly because it increases the risk of stroke. When the upper chambers of the heart quiver instead of contracting normally, blood can pool and form clots, especially in a small pouch called the left atrial appendage. If a clot breaks loose and travels to the brain, it causes a stroke.

People with atrial fibrillation have about a 5 times higher risk of stroke compared with people who do not have the condition. The good news is that medicines called anticoagulants (blood thinners) can greatly reduce this risk.

The risk of stroke depends on other factors besides atrial fibrillation, including:

  • ●Older age
  • ●High blood pressure
  • ●Diabetes
  • ●Previous stroke or heart problems
  • ●Blood vessel disease

Your doctor will assess your individual risk to decide if you need anticoagulants. Other problems related to atrial fibrillation can include:

  • ●Heart failure, if the heart beats fast for a long time and weakens
  • ●Reduced quality of life due to symptoms
  • ●Anxiety about the irregular heartbeat

How is atrial fibrillation diagnosed?

Atrial fibrillation is diagnosed with an electrocardiogram, or "ECG" (a test that records the electrical activity of the heart). Because the abnormal rhythm sometimes comes and goes, you might need a longer recording.

Tests that can help diagnose atrial fibrillation include:

  • ●Electrocardiogram (ECG) – This is the main test. If the abnormal rhythm is present during the recording, the diagnosis is immediate. The test takes only a few minutes.
  • ●Holter monitor – A small device you wear at home for 24 to 48 hours. It is useful if the abnormal rhythm comes and goes.
  • ●Smartwatch or wearable device – Modern devices like Apple Watch or Samsung watches can detect the irregular rhythm. These are useful, but the diagnosis must always be confirmed with a standard ECG.
  • ●Echocardiogram (ultrasound of the heart) – This test shows how well the heart is pumping and if there are other heart problems.

Your doctor will also order blood tests to:

  • ●Check thyroid function (thyroid problems can cause atrial fibrillation)
  • ●Check kidney function (important for choosing the right anticoagulant)
  • ●Measure electrolytes like potassium and magnesium

How is atrial fibrillation treated?

Treatment for atrial fibrillation has two main goals: preventing stroke and controlling symptoms. The most important treatment is preventing stroke with anticoagulants (blood thinners). Your doctor will also decide whether treatment is needed to control your heart rate or rhythm.

What about medicines to prevent stroke?

Anticoagulants are also sometimes called "blood thinners." But they do not actually thin the blood. They reduce the blood's ability to clot, which helps prevent strokes.

Not all people with atrial fibrillation need anticoagulants. Your doctor will assess your stroke risk based on factors like age, high blood pressure, diabetes, previous stroke, and heart problems. If your risk is high, the benefits of anticoagulants greatly outweigh the bleeding risks. Studies show these medicines reduce stroke risk by more than 80 percent.

The main anticoagulants used today include:

  • ●Apixaban (brand name: Eliquis)
  • ●Rivaroxaban (brand name: Xarelto)
  • ●Edoxaban (brand names: Savaysa, Lixiana)
  • ●Dabigatran (brand name: Pradaxa)

These newer anticoagulants are the preferred treatment for most people. They have several advantages:

  • ●No need for regular blood tests to check dosing
  • ●More predictable effect
  • ●Fewer interactions with foods and other medicines
  • ●Very effective at preventing stroke
  • ●Lower risk of bleeding in the brain compared with warfarin

Warfarin (brand name: Jantoven, also called Coumadin in some places) – This is the older anticoagulant. It is still used in some situations, including:

  • ●People with artificial heart valves
  • ●Certain types of heart valve problems
  • ●Severe kidney disease
  • ●People who have used warfarin for years with good results

Warfarin requires frequent blood tests to check that the dosing is correct. It also requires attention to diet, because foods high in vitamin K (like leafy green vegetables) can affect how the medicine works.

Important things to know about anticoagulants:

  • ●Never stop taking anticoagulants without talking to your doctor. Your stroke risk returns immediately if you stop.
  • ●Take the medicine exactly as prescribed. Following the schedule and dose is essential.
  • ●Watch for signs of bleeding. Contact your doctor if you notice blood in your urine, black stools, frequent nosebleeds, or bruising that appears easily.
  • ●Tell all doctors and dentists that you take anticoagulants.
  • ●Before procedures or surgery, your doctor will tell you if and when to temporarily stop the medicine.

What about medicines to control heart rate or rhythm?

Besides preventing stroke, your doctor might treat the abnormal rhythm itself to improve symptoms. There are two main approaches: slowing the heart rate (even though it stays irregular) or trying to restore normal rhythm.

Rate control – If your heart beats too fast with atrial fibrillation, your doctor can prescribe medicines to slow it down. This approach accepts the irregular rhythm but keeps it under control. Medicines commonly used include:

  • ●Beta blockers (like metoprolol, carvedilol, or bisoprolol) – These are the first choice for most people
  • ●Calcium channel blockers (like diltiazem or verapamil) – An alternative to beta blockers in some cases
  • ●Digoxin – Used less often, mainly in people with heart failure

For many people, this approach is enough and allows a normal life.

Rhythm control – If you have many symptoms or the abnormal rhythm started recently, your doctor might propose restoring normal rhythm. This can be done with:

  • ●Electrical cardioversion – A small electric shock to the heart, given while you are asleep with light anesthesia. It is very effective (works in 9 out of 10 people) and does not hurt because you are asleep. It is done in the hospital and takes only a few minutes.
  • ●Medicine cardioversion – Medicines given by vein or by mouth that can restore normal rhythm. They work in about half of cases, especially if the abnormal rhythm started recently.
  • ●Medicines to maintain normal rhythm – After restoring normal rhythm, your doctor might prescribe antiarrhythmic medicines to take daily to prevent the abnormal rhythm from returning. The main ones include:
    • – Flecainide or propafenone (used if the heart is structurally normal)
    • – Amiodarone (very effective but can have side effects with long-term use, so it requires regular monitoring)

Important about cardioversion

If atrial fibrillation has been present for more than 2 days, there is a risk that clots have formed in the heart. For this reason:

  • ●You will need to take anticoagulants for at least 3 weeks before cardioversion
  • ●Or have a special echocardiogram (transesophageal) to rule out clots
  • ●After cardioversion, anticoagulants must be continued for at least 4 weeks, and often indefinitely based on your stroke risk

What is catheter ablation?

Catheter ablation is a procedure that can permanently correct atrial fibrillation in many people. It is especially effective in forms that come and go (paroxysmal) and in younger people.

During ablation, the cardiologist inserts thin tubes (catheters) through a vein in the groin and guides them to the heart. Using heat or cold, the doctor destroys the areas of the heart causing the abnormal rhythm, particularly where the pulmonary veins connect to the left atrium.

  • ●Done under general anesthesia or deep sedation, so you will not feel pain
  • ●Takes about 2 to 4 hours
  • ●Requires 1 to 2 days in the hospital
  • ●Recovery is fairly quick; most people return to normal activities in 1 to 2 weeks

Ablation is particularly recommended for:

  • ●Young or middle-aged people with atrial fibrillation that comes and goes
  • ●People with many symptoms despite medicines
  • ●People who prefer a long-term solution instead of taking medicines forever
  • ●People with heart failure caused or worsened by the abnormal rhythm

The success rate is good: in about 7 to 8 out of 10 people with paroxysmal atrial fibrillation, the abnormal rhythm does not return after a single procedure. Sometimes a second ablation is needed for lasting results.

Like all medical procedures, ablation carries some risks, although they are rare in experienced centers:

  • ●Bleeding or bruising at the catheter entry site
  • ●Fluid collection around the heart (requires drainage)
  • ●Stroke or blood vessel problems (very rare)

Your cardiologist will explain the specific risks and benefits for your case.

After ablation

  • ●First 3 months – A "settling" period. The abnormal rhythm can return temporarily; this does not mean the procedure failed.
  • ●Anticoagulants – Usually must be continued indefinitely if you have stroke risk factors.
  • ●Regular monitoring – With ECGs and Holter monitors to check that the abnormal rhythm does not return.
  • ●Lifestyle – Essential to continue controlling weight, blood pressure, and avoiding alcohol.

What lifestyle changes can help?

Even if you already have atrial fibrillation, lifestyle changes can greatly reduce recurrences, improve treatment effectiveness, and make you feel better. If you do not have it yet, these steps can help prevent it.

The most important factors to control include:

  • ●Blood pressure – Keeping blood pressure under control (target below 130/80 mmHg) is essential. High blood pressure is one of the main factors that cause and worsen atrial fibrillation.
  • ●Body weight – Losing 10 percent of your weight if you are overweight can cut the recurrence rate in half. This is one of the most effective changes.
  • ●Alcohol – Reducing or completely eliminating alcohol greatly reduces recurrences. Even moderate amounts (1 to 2 drinks per day) can trigger the abnormal rhythm. Binge drinking (drinking a lot on one occasion) is especially dangerous.
  • ●Sleep apnea – If you snore heavily and feel tired during the day, you might have sleep apnea. Treating it with CPAP (a mask worn at night) reduces recurrences by almost half.
  • ●Diabetes – Keep blood sugar well controlled if you have diabetes.

Some factors can trigger atrial fibrillation. The most common include:

  • ●Alcohol – Even a single night of heavy drinking can trigger an episode
  • ●Too little sleep – Sleeping less than 6 hours per night increases risk
  • ●Severe stress – Situations of intense emotional stress
  • ●Excess caffeine – More than 4 to 5 cups of coffee per day in sensitive people (1 to 3 cups are fine for most people)
  • ●Energy drinks – Can trigger abnormal rhythms
  • ●Dehydration – Drinking too little water, especially in summer

Physical activity – Regular moderate exercise is good and reduces the risk of abnormal rhythms. The ideal is:

  • ●Walking briskly, cycling, or swimming 30 minutes per day for 5 days per week
  • ●Avoid extreme endurance sports (marathons, triathlons, ultra-cycling); very intense exercise for many hours increases the risk of atrial fibrillation
  • ●If you do competitive sports, talk with your cardiologist for a personalized assessment

What else should I know about living with atrial fibrillation?

If you have atrial fibrillation, regular follow-up with your doctor is important:

  • ●Visits – Every 6 months if you are stable, more often if there are changes
  • ●Blood tests – At least once a year to check kidney function, thyroid, and other parameters
  • ●ECG and echocardiogram – Periodically to see how the heart is doing
  • ●If taking warfarin – Monthly blood tests to check INR

Learning to feel your pulse can help you know if the abnormal rhythm is present:

  • ●Place two fingers (index and middle) on your wrist or neck
  • ●With atrial fibrillation, the beat will be completely irregular, without a rhythm
  • ●If you notice the abnormal rhythm returning, inform your doctor

You can travel safely, even by plane. Some tips:

  • ●Always carry medicines in carry-on luggage
  • ●Bring extra medicines in case of delays
  • ●Drink water regularly during the flight
  • ●Get up and walk every 2 hours on long flights
  • ●Carry a letter from your doctor explaining your condition and medicines

Many people with atrial fibrillation live normal, active lives. Modern treatments are very effective at preventing stroke and controlling symptoms. The most important things are to take prescribed medicines regularly (especially anticoagulants), get regular check-ups, and follow a healthy lifestyle. With proper management, atrial fibrillation is a condition that can be controlled very well.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will I need to take anticoagulants forever?

It depends on your stroke risk. If you have risk factors like older age, high blood pressure, diabetes, or heart problems, anticoagulants should be taken indefinitely, even if the abnormal rhythm seems gone. The stroke risk persists because the abnormal rhythm can return without you noticing it. Your doctor will assess your specific case.

If I have ablation and it works, can I stop anticoagulants?

Usually no. Even after successful ablation, the abnormal rhythm can return silently (without symptoms), so the stroke risk remains. For this reason, if you have risk factors, anticoagulants should be continued. Only in very selected cases and after prolonged monitoring can you discuss with your doctor the possibility of stopping them.

Can I drink coffee?

Yes, moderate coffee consumption (1 to 3 cups per day) is fine for most people with atrial fibrillation and does not increase the risk of abnormal rhythms. Avoid only excessive amounts (more than 4 to 5 cups per day) or if you notice that coffee triggers your symptoms.

Are smartwatches reliable for monitoring the abnormal rhythm?

Modern smartwatches (Apple Watch, Samsung, Fitbit) are fairly reliable at detecting atrial fibrillation and can be useful for monitoring if the abnormal rhythm returns. However, they do not replace medical diagnosis: if a smartwatch detects an abnormal rhythm, it must always be confirmed with a standard ECG by a doctor. They can also give false alarms, so do not be overly alarmed.

If I have no symptoms, do I still need treatment?

Absolutely yes. Even without symptoms, the stroke risk is the same. Anticoagulants should be taken if indicated, regardless of symptoms. As for controlling heart rate or rhythm, if you have no symptoms and the heart is not beating too fast, specific treatment beyond anticoagulants might not be needed.

Can I exercise?

Yes, moderate physical activity is recommended and is good for you. Walking, swimming, cycling, and exercise classes are all safe activities. Avoid only extreme endurance sports (marathons, triathlons), and if you take anticoagulants, be careful with contact sports because of trauma risk. Talk with your cardiologist for personalized advice.

What foods should I avoid?

If you take newer oral anticoagulants (apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban, dabigatran), there are no special food restrictions. If you take warfarin, be careful with leafy green vegetables high in vitamin K (spinach, kale, broccoli): you do not need to eliminate them, but eat them in consistent amounts. Reduce or eliminate alcohol because it can trigger the abnormal rhythm.
